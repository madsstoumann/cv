Mads Stoumann — Lead Web Developer & Technical Evangelist

Professional Overview

Hi, I’m Mads!
I’m 51, married with two kids, an avid reader, and a Lego enthusiast. I’ve spent over 30 years working at the intersection of web development, design, and leadership, focusing on creating accessible, high-performance web experiences. My career spans from hands-on development to mentoring and educating fellow developers. I thrive on finding creative solutions, whether building with code or bricks, and am passionate about sharing my knowledge with the broader tech community.

Career Profile

I started my professional journey at 11, working for Denmark's largest comic book publisher, adapting foreign comics for Danish print formats. By 12, I launched a comics fanzine, editing and publishing over 25 magazines and a book on my favorite artist, André Franquin. During high school, I organized two exhibitions on comic art and gave a graphic design lecture, which sparked my passion for sharing knowledge.

My interest in technology was ignited by a VIC-20 computer, which led me to manage printed catalogs and later develop an IT system for Copenhagen's oldest comic book store. In 1992, I created a Visual Basic app for managing products, customers, invoices and subscriptions — an innovation that replaced their outdated command-line system. By 1995, I developed the store’s first website, which evolved into an e-commerce platform by 1997.

In 1999, I founded my own company, where I offered a wide range of services, including graphic design, web development, app creation, and comic book production. During this time, I lettered over 1,000 comic books, designed and developed more than 50 websites, and built numerous apps. Some of my work was highly specialized, such as the creation of two apps for the Danish Health Authority. These apps streamlined processes for managing medical paper inventories and calculating vaccination risk profiles. My company also designed printed ads, branding materials, and logos for a diverse set of clients, ranging from small businesses to large organizations.

By 2014, my main client was significantly affected by the economic downturn, leading me to join Valtech, a leading global web agency. Over the next seven years, I grew into the role of Technical Director for Frontend Development, where I oversaw large-scale projects and mentored teams of developers. I founded and led Valtech’s "Frontend School," a global initiative that created an interactive learning environment for developers, particularly helping more introverted individuals demonstrate their technical abilities. I also specialized in accessibility and performance optimization, conducting audits and presenting actionable recommendations for websites, informed by my personal experiences with Carpal Tunnel syndrome.

In 2021, I decided to focus on a project that emerged from my time at Valtech. I had developed "OneBlock," an extension for the Optimizely platform, which allowed non-technical users to build rich, visually appealing content components. This tool quickly gained traction and was adopted by major companies such as Telia and Coloplast. Eager to explore how this concept could be expanded to the burgeoning world of headless CMS systems, I co-founded Perfection.dev in 2022. This platform provided a visual preview environment for websites using multiple headless sources, aimed at simplifying the workflow for content creators and developers alike. Despite early success and securing investment, we eventually decided to sell the company in 2024 due to slower market adoption of headless CMS technology than anticipated.

Throughout my career, I’ve maintained a strong passion for sharing knowledge. Writing about development and coding remains one of my greatest joys, with articles featured on CSS-Tricks, LogRocket, and other well-known platforms. My Dev.to page, where I regularly post, has attracted over 27,000 followers, and my work is often highlighted in CSS newsletters and industry discussions.

In addition to my professional work, I’ve created browser.style, a CSS library with over 160 components that use minimal CSS, support both left-to-right and right-to-left text directions, and prioritize accessibility. It’s my passion project, something I love working on, as it combines my dedication to simplicity and inclusivity in web development.
